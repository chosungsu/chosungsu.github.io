### Product Measure Spaces

#### Product Measurable Structure

Given two measurable spaces $(\Omega_1, \mathcal{F}_1)$ and $(\Omega_2, \mathcal{F}_2)$, we aim to equip their Cartesian product with a measurable structure.

The sample space is $\Omega = \Omega_1 \times \Omega_2$. Because the Cartesian product $\mathcal{F}_1 \times \mathcal{F}_2 = \{A_1 \times A_2 : A_1 \in \mathcal{F}_1, A_2 \in \mathcal{F}_2\}$ is not a $\sigma$-algebra, we use the $\sigma$-algebra generated by these rectangles.

---

### Product Measures and Fubini's Theorem

Let $\mu_i$ be finite measures on $(\Omega_i, \mathcal{F}_i)$. Our goal is to construct the product measure $\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$.

Fubini's theorem asserts that for $f \in b\mathcal{F}$ (bounded and $\mathcal{F}$-measurable), the order of integration in the double integral does not matter.

Using the measurability of sections—$\omega_2 \mapsto f(\omega_1,\omega_2)$ is a bounded $\mathcal{F}_2$-measurable function for fixed $\omega_1$, and $\omega_1 \mapsto f(\omega_1,\omega_2)$ is a bounded $\mathcal{F}_1$-measurable function for fixed $\omega_2$—define the marginal integrals

$$
\begin{aligned}
&I^f_1: \Omega_1 \to \mathbb{R}, \quad \omega_1 \mapsto \int_{\Omega_2} f(\omega_1,\omega_2)\,\mu_2(d\omega_2),\\
&I^f_2: \Omega_2 \to \mathbb{R}, \quad \omega_2 \mapsto \int_{\Omega_1} f(\omega_1,\omega_2)\,\mu_1(d\omega_1).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $f \in b\mathcal{F}$, then $I^f_1 \in b\mathcal{F}_1$ and $I^f_2 \in b\mathcal{F}_2$, and

$$
\int_{\Omega_1} I^f_1(\omega_1)\,\mu_1(d\omega_1) = \int_{\Omega_2} I^f_2(\omega_2)\,\mu_2(d\omega_2).
$$

The set function $\mu$ defined by

$$
\mu(A_1 \times A_2) = \mu_1(A_1)\,\mu_2(A_2)
$$

is a finite measure on the product measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$.

Fubini's conclusion remains valid if $f$ is nonnegative or integrable; otherwise, different orders of integration may lead to different values.

#### Construction of Independent Random Variable Pairs

For random variables $X,Y$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbf{P})$, the joint distribution function is $F_{X,Y}(x,y) \triangleq \mathbf{P}(X \le x, Y \le y)$ and the joint law is $\mu_{X,Y}(F) \triangleq \mathbf{P}((X,Y) \in F)$ for $F \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^2)$.

The pair $X,Y$ is independent if $F_{X,Y}(x,y) = F_X(x)F_Y(y)$ for all $(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$. Equivalently, for every $A,B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$,

$$
\mathbf{P}(X \in A, Y \in B) = \mathbf{P}(X \in A)\,\mathbf{P}(Y \in B).
$$

### Countable Product Spaces and Kolmogorov’s Extension Theorem

To construct sequences of independent random variables, we must extend the notion of finite product measures to countable products. Fubini's theorem does not permit infinitely many iterated integrals directly.

Define the product $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}^\infty$ by

$$
\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^\infty) \triangleq \sigma(\mathcal{A}),
$$

where $\mathcal{A}$ denotes the collection of cylinder sets. For a random element $X$, its law is the probability measure $\mu_X(\Gamma) \triangleq \mathbf{P}(X \in \Gamma)$ on $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^\infty)$. The finite-dimensional distributions are defined by

$$
\nu_X^{(n)}(G_n) \triangleq \mathbf{P}((X_1,\dots,X_n) \in G_n) = \mu_X(G_n \times \mathbb{R}^{>n}).
$$

#### Kolmogorov Extension Theorem

Suppose we are given a family of probability measures $\{\nu^{(n)} : n \ge 1\}$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ that satisfy the consistency conditions. Then there exists a unique probability measure $\mu$ on $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^\infty)$ such that

$$
\mu(G_n \times \mathbb{R}^{>n}) = \nu^{(n)}(G_n).
$$

Kolmogorov's extension theorem proves the existence of sequences of independent random variables. If we set the $n$-dimensional product measure $\nu^{(n)} \triangleq \nu_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes \nu_n$, then the family $\{\nu^{(n)}\}$ is consistent. The random variables $X_n(\omega) = x_n$ defined on this space are independent and satisfy $\mathbf{X_n \overset{\text{law}}{=}} \nu_n$.
